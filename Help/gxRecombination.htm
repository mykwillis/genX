Combining Genes to Create New Individuals

Recombination is the method by which genetic information from two or more parent individuals is combined into a single set of genes in their offspring.  Recombination is central to the traditional interpretation of genetic algorithms, as it is the process by which good 'chunks' of genes from different individuals are brought together to form a child.

Recombination Methods

There are a very large number of recombination operators that have been described in the world of acadamia, many of them inspired by natural processes, and many that have little biological inspiration.  Arguably the simplest method is single-point crossover, in which a new individual is created by first copying each gene in the first parent until it reaches a randomly selected crossover point, and then copying the remaining genes from the second parent.

Many varieties of crossover-type recombination operators exist, including some that select multiple crossover points, some that restrict the location of potential crossover points, and so on.

Genetic algorithms have long been used to tackle problems in which the individuals represent a potential solution to an ordering problem, such as a description of cities to visit in the Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP).  The encoding choices that have been used for ordering problems of this nature are problematic for crossover recombination, because it turns out that offspring of individuals encoding ordering problems often do not represent viable solutions.

To deal with these difficulties, researchers developed special recombination methods that are sure to always produce viable offspring.  The most popular is probably Partially Matched Crossover (PMX), which essentially attempts to preserve large chunks of genes in each parent.

Selecting a Recombination Method with genX

genX supports the most popular recombination methods, including single-point crossover, PMX, and others.  You can choose the recombination method to use by setting the RecombinationMethod property of the GA component.
