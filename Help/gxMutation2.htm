Mutation

Mutation is a process that alters a gene in an individual, typically by assigning a random allele.  Mutation is applied stochastically with a relatively small probability, on the order of 0.001 mutations per gene copy. 

The Role of Mutation in Genetic Algorithms

The role of mutation in evolutionary biology and computing is debated even to this today.  While classic texts like Goldberg's see mutation as a way of insuring genetic diversity:

"Mutation is needed because, even though reproduction and crossover effectively search and recombine extant notions, occasionally they may become overzealous and lose some potentially useful genetic material...the mutation operator protects against such an irrecoverable loss." [Goldberg, pg. 14]

There are other contexts in which mutation is seen only as a negative.  The "mutational meltdown" is cited as one reason why a small but otherwise stable population of (natural) organisms may be doomed to extinction under certain conditions.  Genetic algorithms typically don't allow invalid allele values for offspring - they just throw out the invalid indivual and try again - and so the danger of extinction is not a factor in the GA. 

Finally, there are those contexts in which mutation is seen as being an active and necessary participant in the search for better solutions.  Permutation problems such as the Traveling Salesman Problem, for example, often use custom mutation operators with large mutation probabilities for the purpose of reordering the salesman's tour.  It may be argued that this is not really mutation, as it deviates so much from the biological inspiration.

It should be pointed out that if mutation is applied to a population by itself (i.e., without recombination), it results in a random search of the solution space.  Obviously, in different circumstances, the randomness it produces can be beneficial or detrimental, and there does not appear to be a good description of what those circumstances are.

Supported Mutation Operators

The following mutation operators are supported by genX:

  • Bitwise
  • Random
  • Boundary

Bitwise mutation is "bit-flipping," whereby a bit on the chromosome is flipped from 0 to 1 or vice versa.  Bitwise mutation may be used with any encoding.

Random mutation works by replacing one allele in the chromosome with a randomly-selected one from the set of permitted alleles.

Boundary mutation assigns an allele a value which is either the highest or lowest value allowed according to the constraints.  Generally, boundary mutation is useful only when using real-valued genes that have some combinatorial behavior during recombination.  For example, when recombination averages the values of each parent's gene when assigning a new gene value.

 

The mutation operator to use is specified by setting the genX.GA.Mutator property to an appropriate delegate.
